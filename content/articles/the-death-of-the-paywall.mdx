## The Paywall's False Binary

A paywall offers you two choices: pay $15/month for everything, or get nothing. This binary creates enormous deadweight loss — the gap between what something is worth to you and what you're forced to pay.

An article worth $0.10 to you sits behind a $15/month gate. You don't subscribe. The writer gets nothing. The reader gets nothing. Both sides lose.

## The Long Tail of Content Value

Content value follows a power law. A small percentage of articles are worth $5, $10, or more to a specific reader. Most articles are worth $0.001–$0.10. The current subscription model can't capture this distribution — it charges everyone the same price regardless of perceived value.

Micro-payments restore the market. Each article is priced at its marginal cost of delivery ($0.001) — the minimum viable price. Readers pay exactly what each piece of content is worth consuming, not a bundled subscription for content they mostly won't read.

## What Changes When Friction Disappears

At $15/month, the subscription decision involves mental accounting, calendar tracking, and cancellation friction. At $0.001 per article, there is no decision — you just read. The cognitive overhead drops to zero.

This changes reading behavior in measurable ways:
- Readers explore more broadly (no sunk cost pressure to "get their money's worth")
- Writers experiment with shorter, more targeted content
- Niche topics with small but dedicated audiences become financially viable
- The long tail of content creation becomes economically sustainable

## The Writer's Perspective

Under the subscription model, independent writers compete for placement in algorithmic feeds controlled by platforms. Under micro-payments, any writer can publish anywhere — readers pay directly, and quality determines earnings.

A writer with 500 daily readers who each pay $0.001 earns $0.50/day — $182/year — from a niche audience that traditional platforms would never monetize. Add USYC yield on accumulated earnings and the economics become even more favorable.

## The End of the Paywall

The paywall was always a workaround. It existed because payment infrastructure couldn't support per-article pricing at a fraction of a cent. That constraint is gone.

On Arc, the cost of settling a $0.001 payment is less than $0.0001. The payment isn't the bottleneck anymore. What's left is pure content value — and readers have always been willing to pay for that, one article at a time.
